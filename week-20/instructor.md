# Week 20: The AI-Native Developer — Instructor Guide

## Week at a Glance
- Theme: Retrospective, career preparation, and graduation. Students reflect on their journey from zero to AI-native developer, prepare for what comes next, and celebrate what they have accomplished.
- What students ship: A polished portfolio (final pass), a career-ready GitHub profile, a written retrospective on their learning journey, and a plan for what they will build next.
- Estimated hours: 6-10
- Difficulty: 1

## Pacing Guide
- **Day 1-2:** Retrospective. Students review their Week 1 work alongside their Week 18-19 work. They write a structured retrospective: what they knew at the start, what they know now, what was hardest, what surprised them, what they would do differently. This is both a learning exercise and potential portfolio content.
- **Day 3-4:** Career preparation. Polish GitHub profile (bio, pinned repos, contribution graph). Practice the "tell me about a project you built" conversation. Review how to talk about AI-native development in interviews without sounding like they cannot code -- they direct AI to code, which requires deep understanding of architecture, testing, and quality.
- **Day 5:** Graduation. Final presentations (each student demos their favorite project in 3-5 minutes). Celebration. Discussion of what comes next.

## Getting Students Started
- Set a reflective, celebratory tone from Day 1. This is not a heavy build week. This is a week to step back, see how far they have come, and prepare for what is next.
- Common confusion: students feel like this week is filler. It is not. The retrospective builds self-awareness. The career prep is directly practical. The graduation creates a sense of completion that matters psychologically.
- Students should have open: their portfolio site, all project repos, their Week 1 work (for comparison), and a document for writing their retrospective.

## Check-in Points
- **After guide.md:** Verify the student has started their retrospective and is not just skipping to career prep. The reflection is important -- it solidifies learning and helps them articulate their skills.
- **Mid-project checkpoint:** By end of Day 3, the retrospective should be written and the GitHub profile should be polished (professional bio, 3-4 pinned repos, no default profile). If the student is behind, prioritize the GitHub profile over the retrospective -- the profile has immediate practical value.
- **Pre-ship review:** GitHub profile is professional and complete. Portfolio links all work. The student can clearly explain what they built and how they built it. They have a concrete plan for their next project (even if it is just an idea).

## Common Pitfalls
- **Treating this week as vacation.** The difficulty is low, but the work matters. A polished GitHub profile and the ability to articulate their skills are directly career-relevant. Gently push students who are coasting.
- **Underselling their skills in interview prep.** Students may say "I just told AI what to build" which massively undervalues what they did. Help them reframe: they researched, planned architectures, made design decisions, directed implementation, reviewed output, wrote tests, debugged issues, and shipped to production. That is software engineering.
- **Not preparing for the "but can you really code?" question.** Students will face skepticism about AI-native development. Prepare them with honest, confident answers: "I understand the code Claude generates because I review every line. I know when the architecture is wrong. I write the tests that verify correctness. The AI is a tool, like a compiler -- it does not replace understanding."
- **Skipping the graduation demo.** Even if there is only one student, the demo matters. Presenting your work is a skill. Make it happen.
- **No plan for what comes next.** Students who finish a program with no next step often lose momentum. Every student should leave with at least one concrete thing they will build or learn in the next month.

## How to Know the Student Gets It
- The student can articulate their growth in concrete terms: "In Week 1 I did not know what an API route was. Now I have built a multi-tenant SaaS with payments, testing, and CI/CD."
- The student talks about AI as a tool they wield, not a crutch they depend on.
- The student has opinions about how to build software well -- they can critique an architecture, suggest a better approach, identify what is missing from a project.
- The student is thinking about what to build next, not just what they built before.
- **Red flags:** Student cannot explain how their own projects work. Student has no interest in building anything after the program. Student's portfolio links are broken. Student cannot describe their workflow to someone unfamiliar with AI-native development.

## Support Strategies
- For the retrospective, provide prompts: "What is the most complex bug you debugged? Walk me through it." "What would you do differently if you built TeamTask Pro again?" "What tool or concept clicked latest for you?" These concrete questions produce better reflection than "write about your journey."
- For interview prep, do a mock conversation. Play the interviewer. Ask: "Tell me about something you built." "What was the hardest technical decision you made?" "How do you use AI in your workflow?" Give honest feedback on their answers.
- For the graduation demo, set a format: 3-5 minutes per student. Show the app, explain one technical decision, share one thing you learned. Keep it structured so students do not ramble or freeze.
- Make graduation meaningful. Even small gestures matter -- acknowledge each student's specific growth, highlight a moment where they leveled up, express genuine pride in what they shipped. These students built real software in 20 weeks. That deserves recognition.

## Differentiation
- **Moving fast?** Write a blog post about AI-native development that could be published on their portfolio. Mentor another student through their demo prep. Start building their next project and present the plan during graduation.
- **Struggling?** Focus on the GitHub profile and one strong project demo. The minimum they need to leave with is a professional online presence and the ability to talk about what they built.
- **Minimum viable ship:** A GitHub profile with a professional bio and 2 pinned repos. The ability to demo one project for 3 minutes and answer basic questions about how they built it.
